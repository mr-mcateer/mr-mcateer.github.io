Metal Rose

Level 1 Metalwork Project -- Mr. McAteer -- Crescent Valley HS

Design. The simple rose project is a good introduction to layout and using tools. We will be making our own templates to learn the process and develop more skills. You will draw the 6 shapes from pages 2 and 3, get your layout marked, then trace, cut with tin snips and the Beverly shear, sand, shape, and assemble.

Tools (there will probably be more)

  • Calipers -- for drawing circles or arcs on paper or on material
  • Center punch -- used with a hammer and makes a dent in metal before drilling holes
  • Rule / ruler / straight edge -- for measuring distance
  • Tin snips or aviation snips -- cutting sheet metal up to 20 gauge thick
  • Beverly Shear -- cutting sheet metal and some other materials. No round materials!
  • Hack Saw -- cutting solid material held securely by a vice or clamp
  • Drill press -- puts holes in things and removes loose objects like hair and clothing. Watch out!

Procedure

  1. Blank paper time. Use the hole punch to save your drawing if you don't finish during class time.
  2. Lay out the 6 shapes from pages 2 and 3 on your paper. Fit them all on the page so we plan ahead and prevent wasting sheet metal -- extra sheet metal means more for other projects.
    Caliper trick: tape a pencil to one leg of the calipers if you don't have a geometry set. Set the opening to the radius (half the diameter), anchor the point leg, swing the pencil leg around to draw the circle.
    STOP
    DO NOT cut out your shapes until you have teacher approval.Marks are awarded for your layout.
  3. Use the circles to create your petals and other flower parts. Draw them close to the samples on pages 2 and 3, but you can get creative with some shapes. Take your time.
  4. Once you have your mark, cut out your paper shapes. Put your name on each piece so they don't go missing or get mixed up.
  5. Use a felt pen or dark sharpie (blue or black) to trace the templates onto your sheet metal as shown in the demo.
    Layout your paper templates with enough spacing for your aviation snips to swing around easily. 2 cm spacing minimum unless you are a really sneaky cutter. Picture the pathway your snips will take and place templates with that in mind.
    GROUP HINT: cut strips of sheet metal at the width of your pieces plus 0.5 cm of wiggle room. Share the strip with others and trace your templates on it together.
Big idea Pieces should not be permanently attached or welded together until as much sanding as possible has been done. Once the flower pieces are tight together, the inside surfaces are very hard to reach.
