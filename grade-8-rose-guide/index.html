Level 1 Metalwork Project

Metal Rose

A guided, step-by-step build. Layout your shapes, get teacher approval, then cut, sand, and assemble a sheet-metal flower you can take home.

Time: 3-5 class periods Reading level: Grade 7 Difficulty: Beginner
Jump to: What you'll make Watch first Orientation Vocabulary Tools (7) Materials Procedure Big idea Troubleshooting Self-check Sources

What you will make

A small sheet-metal rose with five petals, a center bud, and one or two leaves. Designed flat on paper, traced onto thin sheet metal, snipped out, shaped by hand, then assembled.

2 Words you will hear

Five terms the teacher will use a lot. Skim them now so the demo makes sense.

Layout
The plan on paper before you cut anything. Where each shape goes, how big, in what order.
Template
The cut-out paper shape you trace around onto the sheet metal. Reusable.
Gauge
How thick the sheet metal is. Higher number means thinner. We use 22-26 gauge -- thin enough to snip by hand.
Snips
Heavy-duty scissors made for sheet metal. Aviation snips have color-coded handles for left, right, and straight cuts.
Approval
A teacher signature on your layout before you are allowed to cut metal. No approval, no cutting.

3 Tools you will use

Seven tools, in roughly the order you will reach for them. Read the safety note on each one.

Calipers

For drawing circles or arcs on paper or on material.
Watch: The points are sharp. Keep them flat on the bench when not in use.

Center punch

Used with a hammer. Makes a small dent in metal so the drill bit does not skate when you start a hole.
Watch: Safety glasses on. Keep your fingers behind the punch tip, never wrapped around it.

Ruler / straight edge

For measuring distance. Also handy as a straight edge when you draw long lines.
Watch: Read in millimeters, not eyeballed. 20 mm is double 10 mm, not "about the same."

Tin snips / aviation snips

Cuts sheet metal up to about 20 gauge thick. Color-coded: yellow straight, green right curves, red left curves.
Watch: The cut edge is razor sharp. Bend it under or file it flat the moment a piece is free.

Beverly shear

Bench-mounted lever shear. Cuts sheet metal and some other flat materials. No round materials -- they roll and ruin the blade.
Watch: Move material with both hands flat. Keep fingers at least 50 mm (2 inches) from the blade. Lower the handle slowly.

Hack saw

Cuts solid material that is held still by a vice or clamp. Slow strokes, full length of the blade.
Watch: Clamp the work first, every time. A loose piece will fly. Saw teeth point forward; let the blade do the cutting.

Drill press

Puts holes in things. Fast, accurate, and far stronger than a handheld drill.
Watch: Removes loose objects like long hair and clothing. Tie hair back. Roll up sleeves. No gloves. No loose strings. Clamp the work.

5 Procedure

Five steps to layout day. Read the whole step before you start it.

1
Blank paper time

Grab a clean sheet of plain paper. This is where your whole rose lives until you have teacher approval to cut metal.

If class ends before you finish, use the hole punch and slide your paper into the binder. Do not throw it out -- you will keep working on the same drawing tomorrow.

You will know it is right when: your name is in the top corner and the page is blank in the middle, ready for shapes.
Watch out for: tearing the page when you punch holes. Punch the corner only, not the middle of your drawing.
2
Lay out the 6 shapes

Look at the class demo. Your rose has six shapes: three petal layers (large, medium, small), one center bud, and two leaves. Fit all six on one sheet of paper.

All six shapes on one page. The leaf goes lower so it does not crowd the petals.
Pro tip: Tape a pencil to one leg of the calipers. Now you can swing perfect circles. Set the caliper opening to the radius (half the diameter) you want, plant the pointed leg, and rotate the pencil leg around it.
The caliper-pencil-tape trick. Anchor leg stays still, pencil leg traces a circle.
You will know it is right when: all 6 shapes fit on one page, each is labeled (large, medium, small, bud, leaf, leaf), and there is at least 1 cm of white space between each one.
Watch out for: drawing the petals too small. Make the largest layer at least 60 mm across or the finished rose will look like a button.

Stop. Get teacher approval.

DO NOT cut sheet metal until Mr. McAteer has signed off on your paper layout. Marks are awarded for layout. If you cut early, you lose those marks AND you waste material.

Why this matters: most rose disasters start with bad layout. Once metal is cut you cannot uncut it. The 30 seconds it takes for me to look is the cheapest insurance you will ever buy.

3
After your mark, cut out your paper templates

You have approval. Use scissors (regular paper scissors, not snips) to cut around each of your six shapes. These paper cut-outs are your templates.

Write your name on every single piece. Pieces wander. Names bring them back.

Use the circles you drew to design the petals and flower parts. Your design should look close to the teacher samples, but creative variation is okay -- different petal shapes, different leaf curves, your call.

You will know it is right when: you have 6 paper templates, each with your name on it, stacked together so none get lost.
Watch out for: losing the small bud. It is the easiest one to lose. Tape it to the back of a larger piece if you have to step away.
4
Trace templates onto sheet metal

Lay your sheet metal flat. Place a template on it. Trace around the edge with a felt pen or dark Sharpie -- blue or black. Pencil will not show on metal.

Spacing matters. Leave at least 2 cm (20 mm) of bare metal between templates. The snips need a path to swing through.

Left: shapes packed too close. Snips will run into a neighbor before you finish a cut. Right: 2 cm gaps everywhere -- room for a clean cut.
Imagine the snips pathway. Before you trace, run your finger between the templates. If your finger does not fit, your snips will not fit either.
The snips will trace a smooth curve about 2-3 mm outside your line. Picture that path before you commit.
Group hint: If your group buys sheet metal together, cut long strips first. Width = the widest piece + 0.5 cm of "wiggle room." Share strips. Less waste, less fighting over the good piece.
A shared strip with everyone's templates traced and labeled. Cut order can be any order, but cut full-strip pieces from one end.
You will know it is right when: every shape on the metal has at least 2 cm of bare metal around it, your name is in pen on the metal next to each one, and you have not cut yet.
Watch out for: tracing on the back side. Metal often has one shinier side. Decide which side is "front" and trace on the front, then your sharpie lines disappear when you sand later.
5
Cut, label, and stack

Now you cut. Use tin snips for curves, the Beverly shear for straight runs. Make smooth, slow cuts -- short choppy cuts leave jagged edges that take forever to clean up.

As each piece comes free, write your name on it in Sharpie and stack it with your other pieces. Six pieces total. Count them before you walk away from your station.

You will know it is right when: you have 6 metal pieces, each with your name on it, stacked together. Hand check: count them out loud.
Watch out for: the cut edges. They are sharp enough to draw blood. Bend any rough edge under with pliers before you put a piece in your hand again.
Coming next class

Shaping the petals (curl them with the round end of a hammer), drilling the center hole, sanding every piece with 120 then 220 grit, then assembly with a center bolt or rivet, plus the leaves and stem. The original instructions stop here -- "to be continued."

Big idea -- remember this one

Sand first. Assemble second.

Do not permanently attach or weld pieces together until you have done as much sanding as possible. Once two pieces are joined, you cannot reach the inside surfaces with sandpaper. A rough inside surface stays rough forever, and it is the first thing your grandma will notice when you give her the rose.

6 Troubleshooting

Tap a question to see the answer.

My circles look like ovals. What did I do wrong?

The pencil leg of the calipers slipped while you were drawing. Press harder on the point leg (the anchor) and lighter on the pencil leg. Tape the pencil more firmly so it cannot rotate.

My snips are stuck halfway through a cut.

You probably tried to turn too sharp. Open the snips, back out a few millimeters, and re-enter the cut at a wider angle. For tight curves, switch to red (left) or green (right) snips.

My templates do not fit on one page.

You drew the shapes too big. Erase and start over with the largest petal layer at 60-70 mm across. Everything else scales down from there.

The Sharpie line on metal is wider than my pencil line on paper.

That is normal. Cut on the OUTSIDE of the Sharpie line, not the middle. You will sand the line off later.

I made one shape wrong. Do I have to redo everything?

No. Draw and cut a replacement template just for that shape. The other five are still good.

I cut myself on a metal edge.

Tell Mr. McAteer immediately. Wash with soap and water, apply a band-aid from the first-aid kit. Then bend or file every sharp edge before you keep working.

Can I use scrap metal from the bin instead of new sheet?

Yes -- as long as it is flat, clean, and at least as big as your largest template. Scrap is encouraged. Less waste = better project.
