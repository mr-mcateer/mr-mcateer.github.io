1. Executive Summary

What Is Frisbeam?

Frisbeam is an LED-powered disc game designed for nighttime outdoor play. Think frisbee meets cornhole, but it glows. Players throw illuminated discs at lit-up ground targets, turning any backyard, beach, or campsite into a game venue after dark.

Three product tiers serve different customer needs: Starter ($129), Glow ($199, our flagship), and Pro ($299). All sets feature integrated LED lighting, durable construction, and portable carrying cases.

2. The Product

What we're building, what makes it different, and how the lineup is structured.

What Makes Frisbeam Different

Existing night games fall into two camps: traditional games you can't play after sunset (cornhole, frisbee), or cheap LED accessories (glow sticks, light-up balls) that don't offer real gameplay. Frisbeam is the first product that combines all four of these elements:

Integrated LED System

LEDs are built into the discs and targets -- not glued on, not retrofitted. The glow is the product.

Structured Game

Real rules, scoring, and team play. Not just a glowing toy -- a competitive game you get better at.

Premium Build

Aircraft-grade materials and weather-resistant construction. Built to last seasons, not a single party.

True Portability

Everything fits in one carrying case. Set up in under two minutes at any location.

3. Who Buys This

Sourced from: Psychographic Cohort Map & Jobs-to-Be-Done Profile

Five Buyer Personas

Based on survey data from 1,200 outdoor game buyers, we identified five distinct customer types. Here's who they are and what they care about:

1. The Night Owl 28% of market -- 24% of spend

Who they are: Ages 22-30, urban/suburban, income $45K-$85K. Social butterflies who host gatherings and want their events to stand out.

What they care about: Does it look good in photos? Will it make my event feel special? They spend $89-$129 per purchase.

Biggest worry: "It'll look lame in person or won't photograph well."

2. The Experience Collector (Highest Value) 22% of market -- 29% of spend

Who they are: Ages 25-35, suburban homeowners, income $85K-$150K+. They invest in experiences and quality gear for entertaining.

What they care about: Quality, durability, and "does this feel premium?" Highest average order at $149-$219.

Biggest worry: "Is this a gimmick that'll break after two uses?"

3. The Active Social 25% of market -- 22% of spend

Who they are: Ages 21-32, coastal/college town, income $50K-$95K. Athletic, social, and always looking for the next group activity.

What they care about: Real gameplay and competition. They want a sport, not a toy. Spend $99-$149.

Biggest worry: "This feels like a novelty, not real sports equipment."

4. The Gift Buyer 15% / 15%

Ages 30-50. Buys for others -- housewarming, Father's Day, holidays. Spends $99-$169. Worries the gift will collect dust.

5. The Competitive Player 10% / 10%

Ages 24-38. Tournament-minded. Wants pro-grade gear and cares about game balance. Spends $169-$249. Worries about amateur quality.

Where to Focus

The Night Owl and Experience Collector together represent 50% of the market but 53% of total spending. Our marketing should speak to these two groups first -- LED glow and shareable aesthetics are the top messaging drivers for both.

Why People Buy LED Outdoor Games

Beyond demographics, we studied the underlying motivations -- the "jobs" customers are trying to accomplish when they buy a product like Frisbeam:

What is a "Jobs-to-Be-Done" Framework?

Instead of asking "who is our customer?", this approach asks "what problem are they trying to solve?" A classic example: people don't buy a drill because they want a drill. They buy it because they want a hole in the wall. Understanding the real "job" helps us build and market the right product.

The Core Job

"Help me create memorable social experiences that extend into the night."

LED glow isn't just a feature -- it's the mechanism that unlocks an entirely new window for outdoor play. Every other yard game stops working when the sun goes down. Frisbeam starts working.

Three Types of Needs

Functional

It Has to Work

Sets up in under 2 minutes. Plays in complete darkness. Handles groups of 2-10+. 8+ hour battery. Survives weather and rough use.

Emotional

It Has to Feel Right

Makes the host feel like they elevated the gathering. Creates "wow" moments when the LEDs turn on. Provides a screen-free group activity.

Social

It Has to Look Good

Makes you "the fun friend." Creates Instagram-worthy moments. Gives groups something to do together instead of fragmenting.

What Triggers a Purchase

Four moments when customers are most likely to buy:

  1. Boredom with current options -- "We always just sit around the fire. There's nothing to do."
  2. Seeing it at someone else's event -- The single most powerful trigger. Word-of-mouth in action.
  3. Seasonal shift to warm weather -- Spring planning begins in March-April.
  4. Gift-giving occasions -- Housewarming, Father's Day, Christmas.

Timing Insight

The Thursday-to-Saturday planning window shows the highest purchase intent. People plan their weekend activities mid-week. Our ad spend should be heaviest during this window.

What is DTC?

Direct-to-Consumer -- selling through our own website rather than through retailers. DTC gives us higher margins (we keep more of each sale), direct customer relationships, and control over the brand experience. It's the primary channel for most modern product launches.

This section answers two fundamental business questions: How much do we make per sale? and How much does it cost to get a customer?

What are CAC and LTV?

CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) = the average amount we spend in marketing to get one new customer. LTV (Lifetime Value) = the total revenue we expect from that customer over time. The ratio between these two numbers tells us whether our business model works. A healthy DTC business has an LTV-to-CAC ratio of at least 3:1. Ours is 8.2:1.

The Glow Aesthetic: LED visible but not overwhelming. People are the subject, glow is the atmosphere. Think campfire energy -- the light draws people together.

Site Architecture

The website is designed with a flat structure -- meaning customers can reach any page in two clicks or fewer. This reduces drop-off and keeps the path to purchase short:

Home

Gateway with hero video, social proof, and product preview

Shop

Product detail, gallery, tier comparison, reviews

Our Story

Brand narrative, values, team

How to Play

Rules, scoring, strategy, variations

Primary conversion path: Home > Shop > Cart Drawer > Checkout. Three steps. No account creation required. Apple Pay, Google Pay, and Shop Pay for one-tap checkout.

Mobile-First Design

We expect 70%+ of traffic on mobile devices. The entire site is designed mobile-first with these key patterns:

Thumb-Zone Design

The "Add to Cart" button stays fixed at the bottom of the screen, always within thumb reach. All touch targets are at least 44x44 pixels.

Express Checkout

Apple Pay and Google Pay as primary mobile options. Single-page checkout on mobile. Autofill optimization on all form fields.

Fast Loading

Pages load in under 2 seconds on 4G networks. Zero JavaScript frameworks -- just clean, fast HTML and CSS.

Accessible

WCAG 2.1 Level AA compliant. Keyboard navigable. Screen reader support. High contrast (15.4:1 ratio).

What is WCAG?

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines -- the international standard for making websites usable by people with disabilities (visual, motor, cognitive). Level AA is the standard most businesses target. Meeting it means our site works for screen readers, keyboard navigation, and users with low vision. It's also increasingly a legal requirement.

Performance Philosophy

The Frisbeam site runs on zero frameworks, zero build tools, and zero dependencies. Three static files (HTML, CSS, JavaScript) plus images and fonts. This means:

  • Faster page loads (no framework bloat)
  • Lower hosting costs (static files are nearly free to serve)
  • Better search engine ranking (Google rewards fast sites)
  • Easier maintenance (no complex build pipeline to break)

Key Risks and How We Manage Them

Every product launch carries risk. Here are the four biggest threats to the Frisbeam plan, along with our mitigation strategy for each:

Risk Severity What Could Happen How We Mitigate
Seasonal concentration High 40-60% revenue drop in Q1 during winter Holiday gifting campaigns, indoor play content, pre-season email nurturing
Rising ad costs High Social ad costs increasing 15-20% year-over-year Invest in organic content flywheel, email list, and influencer relationships that reduce dependence on paid ads
Competitor entry Medium Established game company copies the LED concept within 12-18 months Build brand equity and community early. Patent-pending design. First-mover advantage in customer relationships
LED supply chain Medium 8-10 week lead times for LED components, potential shortages Qualify secondary supplier. Maintain 6-week safety stock. Order ahead of peak season

Finalize Product Manufacturing

Confirm LED component supplier, produce initial run of Glow sets for influencer seeding and photography.
