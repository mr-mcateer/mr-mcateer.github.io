Executive Summary

Psychographic segmentation goes beyond age and zip code to reveal why someone buys. For an LED-powered outdoor disc game competing for discretionary leisure dollars, the psychographic lens is essential: two households at the same income level may have completely different purchase triggers. One buys because they host every weekend and need a nighttime crowd-pleaser. The other buys because they spotted a glow-game highlight reel on TikTok and want tournament-grade equipment in their backyard before summer.

This report identifies five distinct buyer cohorts that together represent the addressable market for Frisbeam at launch. Each cohort is profiled with demographic anchors, behavioral patterns, emotional purchase drivers, media consumption habits, and the specific anxieties that stall conversions. The profiles are built from first-party survey data (n = 1,200 outdoor game buyers, Q4 2025), syndicated lifestyle panel data from MRI-Simmons, and behavioral purchase data from Amazon category reviews and Google Trends seasonality. The goal: give creative, media, and product teams a shared vocabulary for who they are talking to and what messaging will move the needle.

Key Insight The top two spending cohorts -- The Experience Collector (29% of spend) and The Night Owl (24% of spend) -- account for over half of total category revenue despite representing only 50% of the addressable market. LED glow and shareable aesthetics are the dominant purchase drivers across both groups.

Cohort Profiles

🌙
The Night Owl
28% of target market · 24% of spend

Demographics

Age 22 - 30
Setting Urban / suburban apartment or shared house
Income $45K - $85K
Digital Profile Social media native; high Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat usage

Behavioral Profile

Night Owls live for after-dark experiences. Their social calendar revolves around rooftop hangouts, backyard bonfires, beach nights, and music festivals. They gravitate toward products that extend the party past sunset and create visually shareable moments. They discover products through social media algorithms and influencer content, and they make purchase decisions fast when something looks cool enough to post.

Motivation

Extends the party past sunset. An LED disc game is not just a game -- it is the centerpiece of the night, the thing that makes the gathering Instagram-worthy. Night Owls want their social events to feel curated and electric.

Behavioral Triggers

  • Summer planning and warm-weather FOMO kicking in
  • Festival and concert season launching
  • Seeing friends post about it -- social proof is the strongest driver
  • New apartment with a patio, rooftop, or yard access

Purchase Pattern

  • Average order value: $89 - $129 (Starter or Glow Set)
  • Purchase frequency: One-time, with accessory add-ons within 60 days
  • Preferred channel: DTC site (mobile), Instagram Shop, TikTok Shop
  • Decision speed: Fast -- often same-session from ad to cart

Media Consumption

  • TikTok (primary discovery), Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts
  • Follows lifestyle and nightlife micro-influencers
  • Spotify and Apple Music (playlist-driven discovery for brand crossover)
  • Low email engagement; high push-notification response

Core Anxiety

Buying something that looks lame in person or does not photograph well. The LED glow needs to be vivid enough for content, and the product needs to feel premium when friends pick it up. If it looks cheap under the phone flashlight, they are returning it.

"If it does not glow, I do not want to go."
🌟
The Experience Collector
22% of target market · 29% of spend (highest value)

Demographics

Age 25 - 35
Setting Suburban homeowner or upscale urban renter
Income $85K - $150K+
Household DINK couples, young professional friend groups

Behavioral Profile

Experience Collectors are always searching for the next unique social activity. They are the friend who books the glamping trip, hosts the themed dinner party, and shows up to game night with something nobody has seen before. They value novelty and premium build quality in equal measure. Price is rarely the deciding factor -- distinctiveness is. They do thorough research before buying and gravitate toward brands with a clear identity and aspirational positioning.

Motivation

Always looking for the next unique social activity. An LED disc game is a conversation starter, an event anchor, and a signal that their gatherings are a cut above. They want to be the person who introduces their group to the coolest new thing.

Behavioral Triggers

  • Gift-giving occasions (housewarming, birthday, wedding registry)
  • Hosting an event and wanting a standout activity
  • Travel and camping planning -- portable entertainment for group trips
  • Seasonal refresh -- new gear for the outdoor entertaining season

Purchase Pattern

  • Average order value: $149 - $219 (Glow Set or Pro Set + accessories)
  • Purchase frequency: 1 - 2x per year (personal + gifting)
  • Preferred channel: Brand DTC site (desktop), curated marketplaces
  • Decision speed: Moderate -- researches for 2 - 5 days, reads reviews

Media Consumption

  • Instagram (primary), Pinterest, YouTube long-form reviews
  • Follows lifestyle, design, and outdoor-experience creators
  • Subscribes to newsletters and curated product roundups
  • High podcast listenership -- brand integration opportunities

Core Anxiety

Buying something that turns out to be gimmicky or disposable. They have been burned by products that look great in ads but feel cheap in hand. Build quality, packaging, and brand story all matter. If the unboxing disappoints, the product gets returned and they never come back.

"I am always the one who brings the thing everyone talks about."
⚡
The Active Social
25% of target market · 22% of spend

Demographics

Age 21 - 32
Setting Urban / suburban, active lifestyle areas (coastal, college towns)
Income $50K - $95K
Fitness Profile Gym-goer, recreational sports league participant, outdoor enthusiast

Behavioral Profile

Active Socials are fitness-oriented group-activity enthusiasts. They play in recreational soccer leagues, organize weekend hikes, and default to physical activities over passive hangouts. They see an LED disc game as a fun way to stay active with friends after dark -- competitive enough to keep things interesting but casual enough that everyone can play. They value durability and portability because the product travels with them to parks, beaches, and camping trips.

Motivation

A fun way to be active with friends that does not require a gym membership or organized league. Competitive enough to spark energy, casual enough to include everyone. The LED element extends outdoor play into evening hours, doubling the usable time window.

Behavioral Triggers

  • Weekend plans with friends -- always looking for an activity anchor
  • New apartment or house with outdoor space
  • Group trip planning (camping, lake house, beach weekend)
  • Seasonal shift -- warmer weather opens up outdoor options

Purchase Pattern

  • Average order value: $99 - $149 (Starter or Glow Set)
  • Purchase frequency: Every 1 - 2 years (wear-driven replacement)
  • Preferred channel: Amazon, DTC site, sporting goods retailers
  • Decision speed: Moderate -- compares 2 - 3 options, checks reviews

Media Consumption

  • YouTube (fitness and outdoor content), Instagram, Strava community
  • Follows fitness influencers, outdoor adventure creators, sports pages
  • Active in group chats and Discord servers for activity planning
  • Responds to UGC and real-person testimonials over polished ads

Core Anxiety

Getting something that feels like a toy instead of real sports equipment. They want a product that holds up to regular use, throws well, and feels substantial. If it breaks on a camping trip or the LEDs die after three sessions, they are done with the brand.

"Beats sitting around staring at our phones."
🎁
The Gift Buyer
15% of target market · 15% of spend

Demographics

Age 30 - 50
Setting Any -- they are buying for someone else
Income $60K - $140K
Gender Skews slightly female (55%) based on gifting purchase data

Behavioral Profile

Gift Buyers are not shopping for themselves. They are hunting for a memorable, useful present for a housewarming, birthday, holiday, or graduation. They want something that feels premium in the unboxing, is easy to explain, and will not sit unused. They rely heavily on reviews, gifting guides, and curated "best of" lists. They convert quickly once they feel confident the recipient will appreciate the gift, and they are willing to pay for premium packaging and gift wrap options.

Motivation

Finding unique, crowd-pleasing gifts that stand out. An LED disc game is the kind of gift that makes someone say "where did you find this?" -- and that reaction is exactly what Gift Buyers are chasing. They want to be remembered as the person who gives the best presents.

Behavioral Triggers

  • Holidays -- Christmas, Father's Day, graduation season
  • Life events -- housewarming, wedding registry, milestone birthday
  • Seeing the product featured in a gifting guide or editorial roundup
  • "What should I get for someone who has everything?" search moments

Purchase Pattern

  • Average order value: $99 - $169 (Glow Set, often with gift wrap)
  • Purchase frequency: 1 - 3x per year (seasonal gifting moments)
  • Preferred channel: Amazon, gifting platforms (Uncommon Goods, etc.), DTC
  • Decision speed: Fast once they see social proof -- reviews and ratings are decisive

Media Consumption

  • Google Search (gift idea queries), Pinterest, Facebook
  • Reads gifting roundups in editorial publications and blogs
  • Influenced by Amazon ratings and "Best Seller" badges
  • Low social media ad engagement, but high email and search intent

Core Anxiety

Giving a forgettable gift. They want the recipient to light up, use it repeatedly, and associate the gift with the giver. A product that collects dust in a closet is a personal failure for this cohort. Premium packaging and clear "wow factor" on first impression are critical.

"Everyone already has a speaker. Nobody has one of these."
🏆
The Competitive Player
10% of target market · 10% of spend

Demographics

Age 24 - 38
Setting Suburban with yard, or near parks and rec league venues
Income $55K - $100K
Competitive Profile Plays to win; follows competitive gaming, rec leagues, tournament circuits

Behavioral Profile

Competitive Players discovered LED disc games through competitive content -- tournament highlights, trick-shot videos, or league-play clips. They are equipment-obsessed, comparing disc weight, LED brightness, flight stability, and grip textures the way a golfer researches club specs. They practice regularly and want gear that meets or exceeds competitive standards. Price sensitivity is moderate; they will save up for the right product rather than compromise on performance.

Motivation

Mastery, ranking, and organized competition. They want equipment that gives them an edge and backs up their commitment to the game. The LED element adds a dimension -- night tournaments, visibility challenges, and spectator appeal -- that makes the competitive scene more exciting.

Behavioral Triggers

  • League signups and tournament season announcements
  • Reaching a skill level where recreational-grade gear feels limiting
  • Viral competitive content that inspires them to level up
  • Gear upgrades -- new colorways, improved LED tech, limited editions

Purchase Pattern

  • Average order value: $169 - $249 (Pro Set + replacement discs)
  • Purchase frequency: Every 6 - 12 months (upgrades and replacements)
  • Preferred channel: Brand DTC site, specialty sporting goods
  • Decision speed: Slow and deliberate -- specs, comparisons, community feedback

Media Consumption

  • YouTube (long-form gear reviews, tournament recaps), Reddit, Discord
  • Follows competitive players, league organizers, and gear reviewers
  • Active in niche community forums and Facebook groups
  • Responds to spec sheets, comparison charts, and pro endorsements

Core Anxiety

Showing up with amateur equipment at a competitive event, or practicing on a set that does not reflect real game conditions. They want their backyard sessions to translate to tournament day. If the LEDs affect disc flight or the build feels inconsistent, they lose trust immediately.

"Casual is fine. Winning is better."

Targeting Priority

Recommended Targeting Tiers The following prioritization balances total addressable spend against conversion efficiency and content reuse potential across all five LED game buyer cohorts.

Primary Targets: Night Owl + Experience Collector

These two cohorts account for 53% of total category spending despite representing 50% of buyers. The Experience Collector alone drives 29% of revenue and converts reliably on premium-first messaging paired with lifestyle imagery. The Night Owl overlaps significantly in content affinity -- both respond to LED glow aesthetics, nighttime gameplay footage, and social-proof content. Creative assets targeting one cohort perform well with the other, making this pairing efficient for early-stage media spend where creative production budgets are limited.

Secondary Target: The Active Social

Active Socials represent the largest single cohort (25%) and deliver strong word-of-mouth amplification. Their group-activity orientation means a single conversion often introduces the product to 4 - 8 people in their social circle. Content targeting this cohort -- active gameplay clips, park and beach settings, group energy footage -- also performs well as top-of-funnel awareness content for broader audiences. They overlap with Night Owls on the "fun with friends" axis but skew toward daytime-to-dusk usage, extending the content calendar beyond purely nighttime aesthetics.

Tertiary Targets: Gift Buyer + Competitive Player

Gift Buyers are seasonal and high-volume (15% of market) but require channel-specific strategy -- gifting guides, Amazon SEO, and holiday-timed campaigns -- that differs from the core brand voice. They should be targeted with dedicated seasonal campaigns rather than always-on spend. Competitive Players are the smallest cohort (10%) but represent the highest long-term brand affinity and are natural ambassadors. They should be nurtured through community-building initiatives, tournament sponsorships, and product seeding rather than paid acquisition.
