Strategic Framework

Why Game Theory Beats "Picking Winners"

Most bracket players try to predict who will win each game as accurately as possible. That is the wrong objective. In a pool with a fixed prize structure, your goal is not accuracy. Your goal is to finish with more points than every other entry. This distinction is critical because it shifts the optimal strategy from "pick the most likely winners" to "pick winners that give you the most expected value relative to the rest of the field."

Game theory (the mathematical study of strategic decision-making when outcomes depend on the choices of multiple participants) tells us that in a bracket pool, the expected value of a pick depends not only on how likely it is to be correct but also on how many other entries in the pool share that same pick. If Duke wins the tournament and 18% of entries picked Duke as champion, the 320 points are shared across many entries and provide limited separation. But if Houston wins and only 3% of entries picked them, those 320 points create massive separation.

The concept is called leverage: the gap between a team's true championship probability (estimated via betting odds and efficiency ratings) and their public selection rate. Teams with positive leverage (underselected relative to their true odds) offer outsized expected value. Teams with negative leverage (overselected) are bad value even if they are the most likely champion.

The Portfolio Approach

With 4 entries allowed, you are not filling out one bracket four times. You are building a portfolio of brackets, each designed to win under a different tournament scenario. Bracket 1 wins if chalk holds. Bracket 2 wins if the analytics-driven sleeper (Houston) breaks through. Bracket 3 wins if a deep contrarian run materializes. Bracket 4 wins if the expert consensus (Arizona) is right. This diversification means you have a realistic path to first place across a wide range of outcomes, not just one narrow scenario.

How These Brackets Were Built

Each bracket was constructed using KenPom adjusted efficiency ratings (which measure points scored and allowed per 100 possessions, adjusted for opponent strength and game location), championship betting odds from major sportsbooks, historical upset frequency data by seed matchup, and estimated public pick distributions. The first-round picks in each bracket are largely similar because seed-based accuracy matters in the early rounds. The brackets diverge strategically starting in the Sweet 16 and Elite 8, where contrarian picks create the leverage needed to win a pool.

Historical Context

What History Tells Us

Championship brackets are not random. Twenty of the last twenty-three NCAA champions have entered the tournament ranked in the top 6 of KenPom's overall efficiency ratings. Twelve of the last thirteen champions ranked top-7 in either offensive or defensive efficiency. This means you can safely eliminate roughly 80% of the field from serious championship consideration before the tournament even tips off.

For 2026, the teams meeting these historical thresholds are: Duke, Michigan, Arizona, Florida, Houston, Iowa State, Illinois, Purdue, and Vanderbilt. Our four champion picks (Duke, Houston, Iowa State, Arizona) all come from this historically validated pool. We are not guessing. We are selecting from the subset of teams that match the analytical profile of previous champions, then using leverage analysis to determine which of those teams offer the best expected value in a pool setting.

Honest Assessment: What Could Go Wrong

No bracket strategy eliminates variance. March Madness is a single-elimination tournament, and single-elimination formats are inherently chaotic. A hot-shooting 12-seed can beat a KenPom top-10 team on any given night. Our brackets are optimized for expected value, not guaranteed outcomes. If a team outside the KenPom top-10 wins the championship (it happens roughly 15% of the time historically), none of our four brackets will have the correct champion. That is an acceptable risk because chasing extremely unlikely outcomes (picking a 7-seed to win it all) has even lower expected value. We are playing the probabilities, not pretending we can predict the future.
