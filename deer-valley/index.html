Trajectory

The Economic Math Is Brutal

  • Racine Olson at $275-350/hr × 2 attorneys. Federal case + settlement + plat fight = deep six figures in legal fees already.
  • 4 lots in Mink Creek ≈ $80K-$150K per lot = ~$400K gross revenue. After dev costs, legal, carrying costs — margin may already be negative.
  • Infrastructure built for 15 lots now serves 4. Fixed costs don't scale down. The per-lot burden is crushing.
  • Every month of delay adds carrying costs on infrastructure serving zero sold lots. DEG may be fighting to lose less, not to profit.

The Political Pressure Evaporates

  • "15 homes for the community" = economic development. Commissioners feel pressure. "4 lots" = one developer's investment problem. Nobody bats for 4 lots.
  • The "jobs and tax revenue" narrative is nearly invisible at 4 lots. The social cost of denial drops to near zero.
  • Deferral risk is LOWER. Commissioners defer when they feel political heat to say yes. With 4 lots, there's almost no heat. They can deny without consequence.
  • The "unreasonable neighbor" narrative loses force. Bim isn't blocking 15 families from homes. He's declining to dedicate his land for 4 lots that may not be viable.

What This Means for the Sequence

  • The Gebos' tipping point is closer than assumed. The wedge between them and Racine Olson is wider. At 4 lots, every hour of attorney time eats into a probably-negative margin.
  • The 7-move sequence works faster at 4 lots. Less political resistance. Less emotional sympathy. Less reason for commissioners to stick their necks out.
  • The reduction pattern itself is evidence at the hearing: "This project has shrunk from 15 to 8 to 4 lots. The applicant's own trajectory tells you this project does not work."
  • Nothing in the legal calculus changes. Condition #6 doesn't scale with lot count. § 50-1308 blocks recording for 4 lots or 400. The federal judgment is the same. All 7 moves still apply.

First Principles: Why 15→4 Is a Gift

The reduction is evidence of a failing project, not a right-sized one. A developer who cuts 73% of their lots is telling you three things: (1) the original plan was built on assumptions that didn't hold, (2) the economic model is deteriorating, not stabilizing, and (3) they are already past the point where the rational move was to walk away — they're in sunk-cost territory, fighting momentum rather than math. Time is dissolving their position. Every month you force them to carry this, the math gets worse. The 7-move sequence doesn't just block them — it runs the clock until the Gebos override Racine Olson and stop bleeding.

The Sequence
7 Moves in Order
This is not a list of options. It is a sequence. Each move sets up the next. Miss a step and the sequence breaks.
1
Immediately — This Week
"The Staff Briefing"
▼

The Action

Nathan delivers a concise, factual memo to the planning director. Not to the Commission. Not to Johnson yet. To staff. The memo contains: (1) a certified copy of the federal judgment, (2) a one-page analysis showing the road dedication upon which access relied has been invalidated, (3) the exact language of Condition #6, and (4) a simple question: "Can the applicant satisfy Condition #6 given the federal judgment? Staff's analysis is requested."

Why This Works

You're not telling staff what to conclude. You're asking them to do their job. They will read the judgment. They will read Condition #6. They will reach the obvious conclusion: the condition cannot currently be satisfied. And they will write that in the staff report.

Psychological Mechanism

Staff now owns the conclusion. It's not Bim's argument — it's staff's independent finding. When Racine Olson stands up to argue, they're arguing against the County's own staff. That's a much harder position than arguing against a neighboring property owner.

Effect on P&Z

Staff report frames the issue before the hearing. Commissioners read staff's recommendation, not your brief.

Effect on Racine Olson

They walk into a room that's already been briefed. The frame is set. They're responding instead of leading.

2
Within 48 Hours of Move 1
"The Johnson Conversation"
▼

The Action

Nathan calls Ian Johnson. Not a formal meeting. A phone call. Professional courtesy.

"Ian, I represent Vawn Smith. You know the federal judgment came down last week. I've provided staff with a copy and asked them to assess whether the applicant can satisfy Condition #6. I wanted to give you a heads up because I know this puts the County in a sensitive position — you were the defendant in the federal case, and now you're being asked to approve a plat that relies on the road the court invalidated. I'm not asking you to take a position. I just want you to know that if this body approves, my client will exercise his rights under § 67-6521. I'd rather not do that. But I will."

Why This Works

Three things accomplished: (1) Johnson knows staff has the judgment (he can't pretend ignorance), (2) the call is framed as courtesy, not threat, (3) the appeal threat is real but personal — "I'd rather not do that" implies reluctance, which implies certainty.

Critical Timing

You MUST reach Johnson before Racine Olson does. If Racine Olson frames this first as "a vindictive neighbor abusing process," Johnson aligns with them. If Nathan frames it first as "the County's own federal judgment creates a compliance issue," Johnson aligns with you. Whoever sets the frame wins. Get there first.

Johnson's Calculation After This Call

"If I advise the Commission to deny, no one blames me — I'm protecting the County. If I stay silent and they approve and lose on appeal, I'm the one who didn't warn them." The math points one direction: caution.

3
10+ Days Before the Hearing
"The Written Opposition"
▼

The Action

File a formal written opposition into the record. Four paragraphs. One page. Nothing more.

Paragraph 1: State the federal judgment — case number, date, holding. The road dedication is void.

Paragraph 2: State Condition #6 — exact language. "Proof of legal 60' access from Autumn Lane to be provided at Preliminary Plat."

Paragraph 3: State the deficiency — the access the applicant relied upon no longer exists. The remaining access is a private easement, 30 feet wide. This does not satisfy the 60-foot requirement.

Paragraph 4: State the consequences — if approved, (a) the plat cannot be recorded under § 50-1308, (b) the decision will be appealed under § 67-6521, (c) the County will be defending another legal proceeding arising from the same road.

Why Four Paragraphs

Commissioners won't read a 20-page brief. They will read one page. Make that one page devastating. No narrative. No emotion. No settlement agreement discussion. Let DEG bring the drama. You bring the facts.

4
At the Hearing
"Controlled Aggression" — The 3-Minute Opening
▼

First 60 Seconds — The Frame

"Commissioners, this is simple. Your own Findings and Order impose Condition #6: proof of legal 60-foot access from Autumn Lane, to be provided at Preliminary Plat. That's your condition. Your language. Your timing requirement. On March 25, a federal court invalidated the road dedication that provided that access. This County was the defendant. This County lost. The access that existed on paper no longer exists in law. The applicant cannot satisfy Condition #6 today. That is the beginning and the end of this matter."

Next 60 Seconds — Close the Escape Routes

"I want to address three things you may hear tonight. First, you may hear that the applicant has broad use rights under a prior stipulated judgment. That's true — but those are private easement rights, 30 feet wide, over my client's land. Private easement rights are not the same as a 60-foot public right-of-way. Your condition requires the latter. Second, you may hear that these issues can be deferred to Final Plat. Your own condition says 'at Preliminary Plat.' Deferral violates your own conditions of approval. Third, you may hear that Idaho Code § 50-1308 is only about recording. That's also true. But the practical consequence is the same: even if you approve tonight, this plat cannot be recorded. My client owns the strip. He will not sign. An approval that cannot be recorded accomplishes nothing for the applicant and exposes this County to yet another legal proceeding."

Final 60 Seconds — The Lock

"My client has asked me to make the following clear for the record: If this body approves the Preliminary Plat without the applicant satisfying Condition #6, my client will seek judicial review under Idaho Code § 67-6521. If this body defers the Condition #6 requirement to a later stage, my client will seek judicial review of that deferral as a departure from the conditions of approval. We preserve these objections on the record tonight. We are not asking this body to do anything extraordinary. We are asking you to enforce your own condition."

Why This Sequence

Opened with THEIR condition (not your argument). Closed the three escape routes. Ended with a locked record that makes appeal nearly automatic. Three minutes. No settlement agreement. No name-calling. Nothing for Racine Olson to grab onto emotionally.

5
During Deliberation
"Object to Everything — On the Record"
▼

The Action

Every time a commissioner proposes deferral: "For the record, my client objects to deferral of the Condition #6 access requirement."

Every time DEG makes a disputed factual claim: "For the record, my client disputes that characterization."

If the Commission votes to approve: "For the record, my client objects to this approval on the grounds that the applicant has not satisfied Condition #6."

Why This Matters

The appeal under § 67-6521 is based on the administrative record. If objections aren't on the record, they can't be raised on appeal. Every objection is a brick in the appeal wall.

Psychological Mechanism on Commissioners

Every time Nathan stands and says "for the record, my client objects," commissioners hear: this is going to court. That fear accumulates. By the fourth or fifth objection, at least one commissioner is thinking: "Maybe we should just deny this and avoid the whole mess."

6
48 Hours After Hearing (If Approved)
"The Title Company Notification"
▼

The Action

Nathan sends a formal letter to every title company operating in Bannock County containing: (1) the federal judgment, (2) proof the road dedication is void, (3) proof Bim has not consented to a plat, (4) a statement that § 50-1308 prohibits recording without the property owner's signature.

The Effect

No title insurance = no closings = no lot sales = subdivision is commercially dead regardless of what the Commission decided. The Commission's approval becomes a piece of paper with no practical consequence. No buyer's lender will fund a purchase without title insurance.

Why the Timing Matters

You do this AFTER the hearing, not before. Before the hearing, it looks like interference and gives DEG ammunition. After the hearing, it's a legal response to an administrative decision. There's nothing DEG or the Commission can do about it.

The Chess of the Threat

The threat of this move is more valuable than executing it. If DEG knows Bim can kill the title insurance, their incentive to negotiate increases dramatically. Nathan can reference "my client's property rights extend to the recording and title process" at the hearing without spelling it out. Racine Olson will understand immediately.

Within 28 Days of Approval
"The Appeal" — § 67-6521
▼

The Brief Writes Itself

(1) County's own Condition #6 required proof at Preliminary Plat. (2) Applicant could not provide proof because road dedication voided by federal court — where County was defendant. (3) Commission approved despite the deficiency. (4) Every objection preserved on the record. (5) Approval is arbitrary, capricious, and an abuse of discretion.

The Psychological Endgame

The Commission knows this appeal is coming (Nathan said so). Johnson knows (Nathan told him). The reviewing judge sees a Commission that approved a plat the applicant couldn't satisfy — after being warned on the record, by the opposing party, and by their own staff report. That's not a close case. That's a reversal.

Pre-Flight Check
5 Things Nathan Must Verify
These are not nice-to-haves. Each one could change the strategy. Any one left unverified is an exposed flank Racine Olson will exploit.
1
What is the correct ordinance number?
Every prior document cites "Ordinance 2024-03." Research suggests this is actually the Stormwater Management Ordinance, not the HSRDP. If wrong, every filing contains an error opposing counsel will exploit immediately.
2
Does the HSRDP actually prohibit private roads?
The actual text of § 2020.040 appears to regulate and permit private roads, not prohibit them. If the HSRDP permits private roads, kill-chain #3 is gone and the entire strategy must be rebuilt around Condition #6 exclusively.
3
What did the County mean by "legal 60' access" in Condition #6?
Review the January 22, 2026 meeting minutes and staff report. Did they mean public right-of-way or just legally sufficient access? This single answer determines whether the Stipulated Judgment's private easement satisfies the condition.
4
Does the Settlement Agreement contain a specific performance clause?
If yes, Fazzio v. Mason (150 Idaho 591, 2011) makes a court order forcing Bim to cooperate a real risk — not hypothetical. Nathan must assess this exposure before filing anything.
5
Has the developer attempted to acquire the strip?
If no: powerful argument they haven't tried lawful means. If yes: the argument shifts to Bim's right to refuse. Either way, Nathan needs the answer to control the narrative at the hearing.
